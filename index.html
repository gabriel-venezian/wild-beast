Gray Wolf

from Canis lupus family
72 kg
13 age

Survivor from the Ice Age, originating from the Upper Pleistocene, around 300,000 years ago. DNA sequencing and genetic studies reaffirm that the gray wolf is the ancestor of the domestic dog.

Gray wolves communicate through a variety of vocalizations, body postures, and scent marking to maintain their pack cohesion.

The weight and size of wolves vary significantly worldwide, tending to increase proportionally with latitude, as predicted by Christian Bergmann's rule. In general, their height, measured from the shoulders, ranges from approximately 23.6 to 37.4 inches.

On average, European wolves weigh approximately 84.9 pounds; North American wolves, 79.4 pounds; Indian and Arabian wolves, 55.1 pounds. Although rare, wolves weighing over 169.8 pounds have been found in Alaska, Canada, and the former Soviet Union.

"Something about the howling of a wolf took a man right out of his here and now and left him in a dark forest of the mind, running naked before the pack."

Gray wolves, also known as timber wolves, are highly adaptable creatures that can thrive in a variety of environments, from dense forests to open plains. They have keen senses, including excellent night vision and a keen sense of smell, making them efficient predators.

Their intelligence and complex social structures contribute to their success as a species, enabling them to coordinate hunts and care for their young within close-knit family units called packs.
